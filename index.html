Research: traits-based macroecology and macroevolution

Functional traits are central to understanding how plants adapt to diverse environments. Trait variation arises both from deep evolutionary history (e.g., phylogenetic conservatism) and from local adaptation to environmental conditions. At the same time, traits influence species diversification and distribution patterns.

My research focuses on how plant functional traits, particularly those related to hydraulic function and temperature adaptation, shape climatic niches (Meng et al., 2022; Guan et al., 2023), geographic ranges (Wen et al., 2018), and the tempo and mode of diversification (Wang et al., 2020). These works seeks to answer questions such as: Why can certain lineages thrive in some environments but not others? Why are some clades exceptionally species-rich? And how do evolutionary processes drive trait adaptation?

Current research directions

1) Evolution and biogeography of xylem elements in angiosperms

Xylem elements form the structural foundation of plant water transport systems, their structure closely tied to hydraulic efficiency, safety, and even gas exchange (Wen et al., 2020). Thus, my research investigates how xylem element shapes hydraulic performance, and in turn, influence the species’ distributions, and diversification trajectories. This is especially relevant in basal angiosperms, where xylem elements underwent a major transition—from tracheids to vessels with scalariform or simple perforation plates.

2) Physiological tolerance to water and temperature stress in subtropical karst forests

Physiological tolerance to water deficits and temperature extremes plays a decisive role in plant distribution (Wen et al., 2018) and responses to climate change (Wen et al., 2023). In karst ecosystems, soluble bedrock drives rapid water and nutrient loss, exposing plants to particularly intense stress (Ning et al., 2025). Focusing on Southwest China, we compare physiological tolerance between species in karst and non-karst forests along temperature gradients, aiming to identify specific adaptive strategies unique to karst plants.

3) Origin, pattern, and dynamics of karst plant biodiversity in southwest China

Southwest China's karst region is a major center of plant diversity and endemism. The interaction between highly soluble bedrock and the humid subtropical climate has shaped unique evolutionary histories, large-scale diversity patterns, and functional adaptations that differ sharply from non-karst areas. My research explores the spatial distribution of plant diversity, endemism, and phylogenetic diversity in the Southwest China karst, and investigates how climatic and geological gradients jointly shape these biodiversity patterns.
